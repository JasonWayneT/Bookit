Here is a comprehensive expansion of the \*\*Genshin Impact\*\* art style:



\## 1. The Core Philosophy: "The Painterly Anime"

While most anime-style games aim for a flat, sticker-like look, Genshin Impact focuses on \*\*Painterly Cel-Shading\*\*. The developers aimed to create a world that feels like a living painting. This is achieved by combining sharp character edges with soft, textured environments that use brushstroke-like details rather than photorealistic textures.







\---



\## 2. Technical Rendering Pillars

To achieve that "2D in 3D" feel, the game utilizes several specific technical artistic choices:



\*   \*\*Custom ILM Maps (Light/Shadow Control):\*\* Unlike realistic games where light bounces naturally, Genshin uses custom "Light Maps" for characters. This allows artists to dictate exactly where a shadow falls on a face (e.g., the iconic triangular shadow under the nose) regardless of the actual light source. This maintains the "hand-drawn" look in 360 degrees.

\*   \*\*The "Rim Light" Effect:\*\* Look closely at characters in the shade; they often have a faint, glowing outline. This "rim lighting" separates the character from the background, ensuring they never get lost in the lush environments.

\*   \*\*SDF (Signed Distance Field) Facial Shadows:\*\* This is a high-level technique used to keep facial shadows smooth and crisp during transitions, preventing the "blocky" or "jagged" shadows often seen in older 3D anime games.



\---



\## 3. Environmental Art: Transcultural Stylization

Genshin’s world-building isn't just "fantasy"; it's a \*\*thematic blend of cultural realism and romanticism.\*\*



\*   \*\*Atmospheric Perspective:\*\* The game uses heavy "fog" and color shifting for distant objects. Faraway mountains aren't just smaller; they shift toward a desaturated blue or purple, mimicking classical landscape paintings.

\*   \*\*Cultural Architecture:\*\* The art style adapts to the region. \*\*Liyue\*\* utilizes traditional Chinese \*Shan Shui\* (mountain-water) aesthetics, while \*\*Inazuma\*\* leans into \*Ukiyo-e\* (woodblock print) inspirations with its sharp, dramatic cliffsides and vibrant flora.







\---



\## 4. Character Design: "The Silhouette Principle"

A key part of the Genshin aesthetic is \*\*Visual Readability\*\*. 



\*   \*\*Asymmetry:\*\* Most characters are designed with asymmetrical accessories (a cape on one side, a vision on the other). This makes their silhouettes unique and dynamic.

\*   \*\*Material Distinction:\*\* Even within a stylized look, the art team uses different "specular highlights" (how light reflects) to distinguish between silk, metal, and leather. You can "feel" the texture of Raiden Shogun’s silk kimono versus Zhongli’s heavy fabric coat.

\### 1. Geometric Foundations

Almost every character and object is constructed using primitive shapes: circles, squares with rounded corners (squat-rectangles), and perfect arcs.

\* \*\*Characters:\*\* The "Kurzgesagt human" is a template. They typically lack fingers, toes, and noses. Limbs are simple rounded strokes (pipes), and eyes are usually two simple black dots. This abstraction makes them universal and easier to animate in high-volume production.

\### 2. Bold Color Theory

The channel utilizes a signature \*\*vibrant, high-contrast palette\*\*. They rarely use pure black or pure white for backgrounds.

\* \*\*Deep Purples and Blues:\*\* Space and "the unknown" are usually rendered in deep indigo or violet rather than black, which allows for better layering of shadows.

\* \*\*Neon Accents:\*\* Biological processes or energy sources often use "poisonous" greens, hot pinks, and electric cyans to draw the eye immediately to the point of interest.

\* \*\*Color as Mood:\*\* In the screenshots provided, notice how "The Universe" feels cold and vast through dark blues, while internal thoughts or "The Ego" are represented with warm, glowing magentas and oranges.

\## Technical Stylistic Elements



\### Flat 2.0: Depth Without Realism

While the style is "flat" (no 3D modeling), it uses several tricks to create a sense of space:

\* \*\*Gradient Overlays:\*\* Subtle, large-scale radial gradients give the scenes a sense of atmosphere. It isn't a hard light source, but a soft "glow" that makes the flat vectors feel premium.

\* \*\*Texture Grain:\*\* If you look closely at the backgrounds, there is often a subtle "noise" or film grain. This breaks up the digital perfection of the vector shapes and gives the art a tactile, paper-like quality.

\* \*\*Hard Shading:\*\* Shadows are rarely feathered. Instead, they use a darker shade of the base color with a sharp edge, often following the curve of the geometric shape.



\### Visual Metaphor and Iconography

Kurzgesagt excels at turning abstract concepts into literal objects. 

\* \*\*The "Brain" as a Circuit:\*\* In your screenshots, the brain isn't rendered anatomically; it’s a series of nodes and paths. 

\* \*\*The "Birds":\*\* While not in every shot, the recurring duck/bird characters act as "universal victims" or "universal observers," allowing the audience to process dark or complex topics (like nuclear war or nihilism) with a degree of emotional distance.

Here is a practical style guide you can use to reproduce a Kurzgesagt‑style visual system (Flat 2.0 / Minimalist Vector Illustration) for your own products, decks, or videos.



\*\*\*



\## 1. Purpose and Design Principles



\*\*Purpose:\*\*  

Convey complex, often abstract or cosmic topics with maximum clarity and emotional resonance, using a highly systematized geometric vector style that is easy to scale and animate.



\*\*Core principles\*\*



\- Geometry first: nearly everything is built from circles, rounded rectangles, and clean arcs.  

\- Flat 2.0, not skeuomorphic: no realistic textures or lighting; depth is implied through gradients, layering, and hard‑edged shadow shapes.  

\- Color as narrative: palettes signal mood, domain (space, biology, mind), and hierarchy.  

\- Poster‑first composition: every frame could stand alone as a poster or infographic.  

\- Emotional buffer: cute/simple characters and birds soften heavy subject matter.



\*\*\*



\## 2. Geometry and Construction Rules



Treat geometry as your “grammar.” If you can’t build it from a basic shape, simplify the idea until you can.



\*\*Shapes\*\*



\- Primitives: circles, ellipses, rounded rectangles, straight lines, simple polygons.  

\- Avoid sharp corners where possible; most corners should be rounded.  

\- Build complex objects by combining primitives (boolean operations) rather than hand‑drawing irregular forms.



\*\*Characters\*\*



\- Head: circle or rounded square, often slightly larger proportionally than realistic.  

\- Facial features:  

&#x20; - Eyes: mostly simple dots or small circles; sometimes a second ring for “glow.”  

&#x20; - Mouth: simple line or small curve; no teeth unless stylized.  

&#x20; - No noses, no fingers, minimal ear detail.  

\- Body:  

&#x20; - Torso: rounded rectangle or pill shape.  

&#x20; - Limbs: “pipes” – long rounded rectangles or simple strokes with rounded caps.  

\- Detail: clothing, hair, and accessories built from large colored shapes, not line detail.



\*\*System constraints\*\*



\- Create a character template that can be recolored and slightly tweaked for different roles (scientist, citizen, alien, etc.).  

\- Maintain consistent proportions (e.g., 2.5–3 heads tall) across all humans to avoid style drift.



\*\*\*



\## 3. Color System and Mood



Color is central to Kurzgesagt’s feel. Define a palette system rather than picking ad‑hoc colors per piece.



\*\*Base palette structure\*\*



\- Space / cosmic: deep indigos, violets, and navy blues for backgrounds. Avoid pure black.  

\- Biology / energy: high‑saturation cyans, magentas, “poison” greens, and hot pinks for focal elements.  

\- Neutral surfaces: slightly tinted darks instead of pure black; off‑white or soft pastels instead of pure white.



\*\*Semantic color roles\*\*



\- Environment base: 1–2 dark hues per scene (e.g., dark blue for space, dark teal for undersea, deep magenta for mind/psyche).  

\- Primary accents: 2–3 high‑saturation colors used for main subject matter (cells, planets, characters).  

\- Warning / danger: saturated red‑orange or toxic green.  

\- Safe / hopeful: bright cyan, yellow, or soft green.



\*\*Mood palettes\*\*



\- Vast, cold universe: dark blue/purple background, bright cyan and white for stars, minimal warm colors.  

\- Inner mind / ego: dark magenta or violet background, glowing pinks and oranges, more warm gradients.  

\- Biological / microscopic: rich teals, greens, and purples with neon accents.



\*\*Rules of use\*\*



\- Avoid using more than \~5–7 distinct hues in a single composition.  

\- Keep value contrast strong between subject and background; subjects are usually lighter and more saturated.  

\- Reserve the brightest colors for focal points and data highlights.



\*\*\*



\## 4. Flat 2.0 Depth, Shadows, and Texture



The style feels premium because of controlled depth, not flat flatness.



\*\*Gradients\*\*



\- Use broad, soft gradients (often radial) across large areas:  

&#x20; - Backgrounds: radial gradient centered on the focal area, creating a subtle “spotlight” or atmospheric glow.  

&#x20; - Objects: simple linear gradients to imply volume (top‑light or side‑light) without realistic shading.



\- Keep gradients smooth and low‑contrast; they should read as “glow,” not as artificial plastic.



\*\*Shadows and highlights\*\*



\- Hard shading:  

&#x20; - Use a single darker flat shape for shadow, clipped to the form’s contour.  

&#x20; - Shadow edges are sharp; no blur or feathering.  

&#x20; - Angle of light is consistent within a shot but can vary across scenes.



\- Highlights:  

&#x20; - Use lighter shapes or thin strokes to indicate edges catching light.  

&#x20; - Avoid specular highlights; think soft, stylized illumination.



\*\*Texture grain\*\*



\- Apply subtle noise/grain overlays to break up pure flat color:  

&#x20; - Low opacity (5–15%).  

&#x20; - Either per‑layer (e.g., just the background) or as a global overlay.  

\- Ensure grain scale is consistent; avoid huge “dots” on small objects.



\*\*\*



\## 5. Iconography and Visual Metaphor



Kurzgesagt visuals lean heavily on metaphor to make abstractions concrete.



\*\*Icon design\*\*



\- Build icons from the same geometric primitives and color system as characters.  

\- Simplify to the essential silhouette—icons should be legible at very small sizes.  

\- Avoid outline‑only icons; most icons are solid shapes with minimal inner detail.



\*\*Metaphor patterns\*\*



\- Mind/brain: circuitry, networks, nodes and edges rather than anatomical realism.  

\- Society: simplified city skylines, crowds as repeated character silhouettes.  

\- Energy/information: beams, rays, pulses, glowing orbs, radiating rings.  

\- Threats: spikes, jagged shapes, high‑contrast red/green combos.



\*\*Systematize metaphors\*\*



\- Create a mapping of abstract concepts → reusable metaphors (e.g., “ego” = glowing core inside a silhouette; “risk” = cracked sphere; “knowledge” = radiating icon).  

\- Keep these metaphors consistent across pieces so viewers subconsciously learn your visual language.



\*\*\*



\## 6. Layout, Composition, and Typography



Think “animated poster”: each frame is composed with a strong focal point and clear hierarchy.



\*\*Composition\*\*



\- Strong focal point:  

&#x20; - Use scale, color contrast, and lighting to make the main idea dominant.  

&#x20; - Place it near the center or at a rule‑of‑thirds intersection.



\- Scale and negative space:  

&#x20; - Use extreme scale differences to communicate magnitude (tiny human vs enormous circle representing universe size).  

&#x20; - Allow large areas of empty or subtly textured space to emphasize vastness.



\- Layers and parallax:  

&#x20; - Foreground, midground, background separated by scale and value.  

&#x20; - Background elements desaturated and darker; foreground elements lighter/clearer.



\*\*Info‑graphic hierarchy\*\*



\- Use clear grouping: clusters of related elements framed by lines, boxes, or subtle background panels.  

\- Numeric or categorical information often gets:  

&#x20; - Big numbers in heavy weight type.  

&#x20; - Simple charts built from rectangles and circles.



\*\*Typography\*\*



\- Font choice: rounded, friendly sans‑serifs (e.g., Quicksand‑like), matching the softness of shapes.  

\- Hierarchy:  

&#x20; - Headline: large, often all‑caps or small caps, high contrast with background.  

&#x20; - Subhead: smaller, still bold or semi‑bold.  

&#x20; - Body: short phrases or sentences; avoid paragraphs when possible.



\*\*Type usage rules\*\*



\- Align text with key shapes (along arcs, centered on circles, or anchored to important objects).  

\- Keep line lengths short; think “caption” not “essay.”  

\- Limit the number of different font sizes per frame to maintain order.



\*\*\*



\## 7. Lighting, Bloom, and “Cinematic” Effects



Even though it’s 2D, lighting is treated cinematically.



\*\*Bloom and glow\*\*



\- Add soft outer glows to bright objects (e.g., “third eye”, energy beams, stars).  

\- Use additive blending or light overlays to simulate lens bloom.  

\- Keep glow radius proportional to object size, and avoid overpowering the core shape.



\*\*Atmospheric effects\*\*



\- Gradients and fog layers: large, low‑opacity color fields that create depth and separation.  

\- Particles: small dots or tiny shapes around focal areas to imply energy, dust, or magic.  

\- Light rays: soft, triangular or trapezoidal shapes with low opacity, emanating from a point or line.



\*\*\*



\## 8. Tone and Narrative Framing



The emotional tone is distinctive: cheerful, cute design paired with existential themes.



\*\*Tone\*\*



\- Visual optimism: bright, saturated palettes, friendly characters, smooth motion.  

\- Subject matter realism: scripts don’t shy from death, cosmic insignificance, or catastrophe.  

\- Result: “optimistic nihilism”—viewers feel informed and strangely comforted, not crushed.



\*\*Supporting this in visuals\*\*



\- Use friendly character expressions even in heavy contexts (concerned, surprised, curious, but rarely gruesome).  

\- When showing disaster or death, use abstraction (silhouettes, icons breaking, worlds cracking) instead of graphic realism.  

\- Maintain a consistent narrator “voice” in on‑screen text: clear, calm, slightly playful.



\*\*\*



\## 9. Production System and Reuse



To make this style practical at scale (slides, videos, playbooks), treat it like a full design system.



\*\*Asset libraries\*\*



\- Core elements:  

&#x20; - Character rigs (head, body, limbs, emotion sets).  

&#x20; - Scene kits (space backgrounds, planets, cityscapes, labs).  

&#x20; - Data shapes (bars, circles, rings, grids).  

&#x20; - FX pack (glows, beams, particles, gradients, noise overlays).



\- Store as components/symbols in your tool (Figma, Illustrator, After Effects, etc.) for easy reuse.



\*\*Template scenes\*\*



\- Create a set of reusable layouts:  

&#x20; - “Big number + tiny human + huge circle” for scale.  

&#x20; - “Three‑panel comparison” for option tradeoffs.  

&#x20; - “Timeline with milestones” for history or process.  

&#x20; - “Radial map” for systems with a central concept.



\*\*Animation‑aware design\*\*



\- Design with motion in mind:  

&#x20; - Build objects in layers that can move independently (foreground, labels, characters).  

&#x20; - Avoid tiny, fussy details that won’t be visible during motion or on small screens.



\*\*\*



\## 10. Kurzgesagt‑Style Checklist



When you create a new visual, evaluate it against this list:



\- Is everything built primarily from circles, rounded rectangles, and simple polygons?  

\- Does the palette use high‑saturation colors with a clearly defined background mood?  

\- Are shadows hard‑edged and gradients broad and subtle (no pseudo‑3D rendering)?  

\- Is there subtle grain or texture preventing perfectly flat “sterile” color?  

\- Does the composition have a strong focal point and clear hierarchy (poster‑like)?  

\- Is typography rounded, friendly, and minimal, with short, clear text?  

\- Are metaphors simple and literal, turning abstract ideas into concrete icons?  

\- Does the tone feel emotionally buffered—cute/optimistic visuals for heavy ideas?



Priority 3: Genshin Impact 

Here’s a style guide you can use to reproduce a “Genshin‑like” art direction, focusing on principles and implementation more than engine‑specific tricks.



\*\*\*



\## 1. Core Philosophy: Painterly Anime in 3D



\*\*Design goal:\*\*  

Create a world that feels like a hand‑painted fantasy illustration where anime characters live inside a soft, luminous landscape rather than on top of a flat backdrop.



\*\*Guiding principles\*\*



\- Painterly cel‑shading: characters read as 2D anime drawings from any angle, while environments feel like brush‑painted illustrations.  

\- Non‑photorealistic rendering (NPR): every rendering decision favors clarity, mood, and stylization over physical accuracy.  

\- Fantasy realism: believable light and atmospherics applied to impossible designs (floating islands, exaggerated architecture).



When you design assets, always ask: “Would this look like an illustration from an art book if I froze the frame?”



\*\*\*



\## 2. Character Rendering Style



This is the heart of the “2D in 3D” look.



\### 2.1 Shading model



\- Cel‑shading tiers:  

&#x20; - Base color (flat).  

&#x20; - 1–2 shadow bands with clear, crisp edges (not smooth gradients).  

&#x20; - Optional highlight band on shiny materials (metal, glass, some cloth).



\- Custom light/shadow control:  

&#x20; - Use artist‑authored masks or maps to define where facial and body shadows fall (e.g., cheek shadows, triangular nose shadow), so shading stays “on model” no matter the light direction.  

&#x20; - Avoid letting dynamic lighting create uncontrolled shapes on faces.



\- SDF/smooth shadow transitions:  

&#x20; - Wherever possible, interpolate shadow edges smoothly in motion to prevent popping or jittering.  

&#x20; - Keep break lines stable on key facial landmarks (nose bridge, jawline, eye sockets).



\### 2.2 Rim lighting



\- Purpose: separate characters from lush backgrounds and add a magical edge.  

\- Implementation guidelines:  

&#x20; - Thin, soft rim along the silhouette on the side opposite the main light.  

&#x20; - Color the rim with a slightly brighter, more saturated tint of the ambient color or the character’s elemental color.  

&#x20; - Keep intensity subtle; the rim should read as a halo, not an outline stroke.



\### 2.3 Material definition



Even within cel‑shading, different materials should \*feel\* different.



\- Skin:  

&#x20; - Soft, matte; minimal highlights; warm undertones.  

&#x20; - Avoid strong specular on faces; keep them illustration‑like.



\- Cloth:  

&#x20; - Varies by type:  

&#x20;   - Silk/satin: sharper, brighter highlight band; subtle color shift.  

&#x20;   - Heavy fabric: broader, softer shadows; weaker specular.



\- Metal:  

&#x20; - Strong, bright highlights; increased contrast between light and shadow bands.  

&#x20; - Sometimes colored reflections to tie into the environment or element.



\- Leather/boots/belts:  

&#x20; - Moderately glossy; subtle highlights with slightly broader falloff than metal.



Keep shader complexity focused on \*shape readability\* and material feel, not physical accuracy.



\*\*\*



\## 3. Character Design Language



Think “silhouette-first anime with ornate detailing.”



\### 3.1 Silhouette principle



\- Each character should be recognizable in solid black:  

&#x20; - Asymmetric clothing (cape on one side, weapons, hair ornaments, or elemental visions) to break symmetry.  

&#x20; - Distinct head + hair shapes (spikes, twin tails, buns, hats, horns).  

&#x20; - Signature props (weapon, book, floating accessory).



\- Pose and posture:  

&#x20; - They should read well in idle stance (no reliance on extreme posing to be identifiable).  

&#x20; - Favor graceful, clearly readable lines of action.



\### 3.2 Layered costume design



\- Multi‑layer outfits: undershirts, coats, belts, sashes, skirts, armor bits, ribbons.  

\- Visual hierarchy:  

&#x20; - 1–2 dominant shapes/areas (e.g., large coat, big cape).  

&#x20; - 2–4 secondary elements (belts, gloves, boots).  

&#x20; - Small decorative motifs (embroidery, emblems, charms).



\- Motif consistency:  

&#x20; - Tie motifs to region/element (geo shapes, florals, calligraphic lines, etc.).  

&#x20; - Reuse patterns and shapes across characters from the same region.



\### 3.3 Color and element identity



\- Limited palette per character: 1 main color, 1–2 secondary, and a neutral.  

\- Use color to encode:  

&#x20; - Element (electro, pyro, hydro, etc.)  

&#x20; - Nation/region affiliation.  

\- Accents: small areas of bright, saturated color (vision, gem, trim) to draw the eye.



\*\*\*



\## 4. Environmental Art and Atmosphere



The world should feel like a romantic landscape painting you can walk through.



\### 4.1 Painterly texturing



\- Hand‑painted or brush‑styled textures:  

&#x20; - Soft edges, visible but subtle brush strokes; avoid photographic detail.  

&#x20; - Large shapes of color rather than noisy detail; major forms get attention, not tiny patterns.



\- Surfaces:  

&#x20; - Rocks: stylized planes with painted color shifts instead of realistic rock noise.  

&#x20; - Grass and foliage: broad color masses plus clusters of simplified blades/leaves.  

&#x20; - Water: smooth color gradients with stylized highlights and reflections, not physically accurate caustics.



\### 4.2 Atmospheric perspective



\- Distance treatment:  

&#x20; - Near: sharper edges, more contrast, richer color.  

&#x20; - Mid: less contrast, slightly cooler or lighter.  

&#x20; - Far: strongly desaturated, shifted toward blue/purple; low detail.



\- Fog and volumetrics:  

&#x20; - Soft height‑fog to separate layers of terrain.  

&#x20; - Colored fog tuned per region (warm for deserts, cool for mountains, pastel for mystical zones).



\### 4.3 Cultural stylization



\- Each region anchored in an art tradition or cultural reference, then romanticized:  

&#x20; - “Shan shui”-inspired mountains, terraced cliffs, stylized roofing, pagodas.  

&#x20; - “Ukiyo‑e”-like shapes and color groupings for coastal rocks, waves, and trees.  

\- Architecture: simplified but ornate—clean silhouettes with repeatable motifs and decorative trim.



\*\*\*



\## 5. Lighting, Mood, and Time of Day



Lighting is used to underscore mood rather than chase realism.



\- Global illumination feel:  

&#x20; - Soft, cohesive ambient light; minimal harsh contrasts.  

&#x20; - Colors of sky and environment bleed gently into objects.



\- Day/night cycles:  

&#x20; - Day: bright, slightly warm; clear color separation.  

&#x20; - Sunset: strong warm gradients; sky and terrain tinted orange/pink; long soft shadows.  

&#x20; - Night: deep blues/purples; bright, saturated point lights (lamps, magic, fireflies).



\- Point lights and VFX:  

&#x20; - Elemental effects use semi‑transparent, additive layers to feel magical and ethereal.  

&#x20; - Avoid heavy, smoky opacity that would feel realistic and dirty; keep things luminous and clean.



\*\*\*



\## 6. UI and Overall Presentation



Even UI and presentation should match the painterly anime fantasy.



\- UI shapes:  

&#x20; - Soft panels with subtle borders, flourishes, and region/element motifs.  

&#x20; - Rounded corners, minimal hard‑edged rectangles.



\- UI colors:  

&#x20; - Light, parchment‑like backgrounds with gentle gradients.  

&#x20; - Accent colors keyed to element or region when relevant.



\- Typography:  

&#x20; - Clean, legible sans or serif with slight flourishes.  

&#x20; - Maintain good contrast but avoid stark, modern “tech” vibes.

For internal reference when balancing your own direction:



| Dimension            | Genshin‑like Painterly Anime          | Urban/Neon NPR (NTE‑like)              |

|----------------------|----------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|

| Primary vibe         | Pastoral fantasy, nature‑forward      | Urban surrealism, industrial           |

| Texturing            | Brush‑painted, soft, organic          | Sharper, metallic, synthetic           |

\## 8. Implementation Checklist



When you design a character, scene, or frame in this style, review:



\- Characters  

&#x20; - Cel‑shaded with 1–2 shadow bands, stable face shadows.  

&#x20; - Clear silhouette with asymmetry and strong hair/prop shapes.  

&#x20; - Distinct material feel for skin, cloth, metal, and leather.



\- Environments  

&#x20; - Hand‑painted, brushy feel; no photoreal textures.  

&#x20; - Atmospheric perspective clearly visible.  

&#x20; - Architecture and nature shapes consistent with the region’s theme.



\- Rendering  

&#x20; - Rim light subtly separating characters from backgrounds.  

&#x20; - Elemental effects luminous and semi‑transparent, not gritty.  

&#x20; - Lighting supporting mood more than strict realism.
